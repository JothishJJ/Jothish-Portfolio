I was watching *The Social Network* — a movie about college students building a billion-dollar company.
Somewhere between the late-night coding montages and the raw energy of building something from scratch, **inspiration surged through me**.
That was when I got seriously into coding — and more importantly, when I realized how powerful a single idea can be when you're curious enough to chase it.
Inspiration matters. It’s what creates movements. It’s what produces a new kind of entrepreneur or engineer, someone who follows their own path and still ends up building something that matters.

In India, engineering isn’t underrated in popularity, but it *is* deeply misunderstood in spirit. We treat it like a safety net, not a superpower. But real engineering is about curiosity, creation, and solving problems that matter.

If you took up engineering to please someone else, maybe your parents, maybe society: that’s probably a bad call. Engineering should be something you choose because you're inspired by science and technology, because you're *curious* and you want to **build**. If you don't feel that way, it's okay but you shouldn’t fake it.

I was wandering around MIT OCW (OpenCourseWare), and I ended up on their admissions page. One line stood out:
"Here, students hurry to class yammering about quantum computing, CRISPR gene editing, and nuclear fusion — sometimes in the same conversation."
This line hit me hard. Maybe it’s because I’m a nerd, but the idea of an environment like that buzzing with people talking science, future tech, and impossible problems, that’s powerful. It made me wonder how many people out there would actually find this exciting. And if they don’t, is it because they were **never truly exposed** to what science *really* is?
If you’ve never been interested in science, it might not be because you lack curiosity but because the system never showed you the *magic* of it. In India, too many students give up on science not out of passion for something else but because they were told it’s ‘too hard’ or ‘only for toppers.’

I want people to choose other subjets apart from science, for their love for learning about those things, not because they’re easier just because they’re inherently passionate or curious about.

Let’s face it: in India, most people see education not as a way to understand the world, but as a **tool to make money**. And I get it. If your family’s depending on you, if you're the first one in your family going to college, you don’t have the luxury of “exploring your interests.” You're forced to think practically. You're taught to chase marks, not meaning.
But here's the thing: if you focus on **truly learning** let’s say, physics instead of just trying to pass the exam, you’ll **actually do better**. Not just in the exam, but in your job, in your thinking, in your life. Because when you learn with curiosity, the marks and the success often follow on their own.

### Now Let’s Talk About the Education System

No blog about education in India is complete without trashing the system a bit. And honestly, it **deserves it**.

I asked my friend once, “If money wasn’t a problem, what would you do?”

He said, “I’d still work. And travel a lot.”

I asked, “Would you study?”

He replied instantly, “Eww, no.”

Then I clarified: “Not college, just reading about technology, culture, ideas…”

He paused and said, “Yeah, maybe I would.”

That hit me. Here’s someone who isn’t against learning. He’s just been **conditioned** to associate it with pain and pressure. Somewhere along the line, he like millions of others  became a victim of what I call **de-curiatisation**: a system that slowly kills your curiosity and turns learning into a chore.

Most colleges in India hand out degrees, not education. The lectures are outdated, labs are dysfunctional, and students graduate without the skills to build, design, or innovate. This creates a dangerous illusion that a degree equals readiness. But real learning requires curiosity, experimentation, and failure none of which our current system rewards.

That’s what our system does. And if you’re reading this, I hope it hasn’t done that to you. Or at least, **not permanently**. Because learning when done right is one of the most beautiful things you can experience. It gives you power. It makes you dangerous, in the best way.
