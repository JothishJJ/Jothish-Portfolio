I'm looking to be motivated in my studies. I'm impressed by the lack of good knowledge in this. I hated studying, but I don't accept that some people are better or “born” for science. After all, if I can learn about complex coding in a week that took months for others, even as a teenager, there has to be something that separates people from studying science.

I was reading a book called Drive by Daniel. H. Pink, still haven't finished it yet. But, from the parts that I read, autonomy is a big factor. We don't get autonomy in formal education, I mean, why is it that when we're expected to stay inside a classroom, without moving, no talking, and get the exact information from the teacher, nothing more, nothing less? Now that I look at it does look like a prison. But when expected to play in school, no one is watching us, sure, there might be one person in charge of a group of students, even so, most of the time they can do whatever game they want, they can run, jump, laugh, talk and so on. How can we expect students to like studying when the contrast is just so much better? Leaving no room for creativity is a big issue with schools. 

In Kerala, schools have a big issue with drugs. There are several programs done now, feel like every month, instead of doing these useless programs that I know students use as an opportunity to leave the class. Instead, they could fix what's fundamentally wrong, and in Kerala's state board schools, classes 11 and 12 have to sit for almost 8 hours every day.

Anyway, I think these are the problems that I have found. A possible way to fix it would, of course would be giving autonomy to students, easier said than done. If it were a few years before, I would have looked at science as boring, too. How will we get those students to enjoy science? For me, I found that my ego never losing was an advantage, since I couldn't let people be “better” than me. (I'm not saying people who know science are better, but just using the idea.) And whenever I get stuck, it's also important for my ego not to give up on that problem. I found that when I'm coding (which I enjoy), I spend hours solving more and more problems, saying to myself, “Just a few more minutes, I'll solve this, it's easy”. Of course, I sometimes spend too much time as well.

I would also like to say that, instead of giving an authoritarian attitude to students. Instead of enforcing rules and saying “It's how it's supposed to be”, give kids the opportunity to think why it should be. Even Einstein, who found Germany's authoritative schools to be very displeasing, found that when he moved to Italy, he found much more importance to education. So why not just learn from the best?
